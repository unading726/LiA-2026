### Ⅰ . Primary SDG Identification
* Which specific SDG number and name does your project most directly support?
* What is the precise target within this goal that matches your workflow?

**SDG 3 - Good Health and Wellbeing** 
*Target 3.d: strengthen the capacity for early warning, risk reduction, and management of national and global health risks.* 

Regular exposure to ionizing radiation poses long-term chronic health risks (such as carcinogenesis) to hospital staff. A safety audit in line with the Chinese national standard on treatments and diagnostics involving ionizing radiation ensures that risk-reduction protocols are actively preventing long-term illness in employees. Because township hospitals often lack specialized radiation safety personnel, our long-term strategies directly build institutional capacity under **Target 3.d**, giving these facilities a sustainable framework for early warning and ongoing health risk management.

SDG 8 - Decent Work and Economic Growth
*Target 8.8: protect labour rights and promote safe and secure working environments for all workers.*

Our audit workflow directly supports **Target 8.8** by evaluating the daily, localised working conditions of healthcare workers in township hospitals. Township facilities frequently face tighter budgets, making them vulnerable to gaps in physical protective infrastructure. By auditing the availability of personal protective equipment (PPE), the structural integrity of room shielding, and the administrative tracking of personal dosimeter badges, we ensure that the labor rights of rural medical workers to a secure environment are protected. The long-term strategies we develop will provide cost-effective, practical safety protocols that help under-resourced hospitals sustain a safe workplace over time.

SDG 12 - Responsible Consumption and Production 
*Target 12.4: Achieve the environmentally sound management of chemicals and all wastes throughout their life cycle to minimize their adverse impacts on human health and the environment.*

Radioactive diagnostics and treatments inherently generate highly hazardous, short- and long-lived nuclear medicine waste. Our workflow aligns with **Target 12.4** by auditing the lifecycle handling of these radionuclides—from safe storage upon arrival to the strictly controlled decay-in-storage protocols before final disposal. In township settings, lack of standardized waste management can lead to accidental exposure for hospital cleaning staff or localized environmental contamination. The long-term strategic frameworks produced by our project will establish standardized, compliant waste management workflows that minimize the release of hazardous materials into the surrounding community and ecosystem.

### Ⅱ . Alignment Logic
* How does your project contribute to this SDG in day-to-day practice?

Our project translates the high-level objectives of SDGs 3, 8, and 12 into concrete, daily operational habits within township hospitals through a two-step approach: **immediate corrective auditing** and **templated long-term strategies**.

- **Daily Safety Habits (SDG 8):** The audit establishes clear, daily checklists for staff before they enter a radiation zone. In day-to-day practice, this means standardizing the habit of checking room warning lights, verifying that lead aprons and thyroid shields are undamaged, and ensuring that personal dosimeter badges are worn correctly on every single shift rather than being left in drawers.
    
- **Proactive Exposure Monitoring (SDG 3):** Instead of viewing radiation safety as an afterthought, our long-term strategies implement a daily "ALARA" (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) workflow. This includes optimizing patient positioning times and using standardized distance markers. It turns vague national guidelines into a daily routine that minimizes the scatter radiation dose a technician absorbs during a routine X-ray or CT scan.
    
- **Standardized Waste Routines (SDG 12):** On a day-to-day basis, the project introduces strict segregation protocols at the immediate point of use. Radioactive waste will no longer risk being mixed with standard medical waste. We provide practical, daily logging templates for "decay-in-storage" rooms, ensuring that spent radionuclides are locked, labeled with their half-life dates, and safely isolated every single afternoon.


* Who directly benefits from this contribution on the ground?

The primary beneficiaries on the ground are the individuals living and working within the township ecosystem, who are often underserved compared to their urban counterparts:

1. **Frontline Medical Workers (The Primary Beneficiaries):** Radiologists, nuclear medicine technicians, and oncology nurses. They benefit directly because their daily cancer and tissue-damage risks are actively minimized. They gain the peace of mind that their workplace is actively monitoring their cumulative lifetime radiation doses.
    
2. **Ancillary and Support Staff:** Hospital janitors, waste disposal handlers, and maintenance workers. These individuals often have less formal radiation training but face high accidental exposure risks if radioactive waste or contaminated items are improperly handled. Standardized daily segregation directly protects them from unknowing exposure.
    
3. **The Local Township Community and Patients:** Patients receiving diagnostics benefit from calibrated, safer machinery with minimized scatter radiation. Furthermore, because township hospitals are deeply embedded in rural communities, proper daily radioactive waste management prevents local soil and water tables from contamination, safeguarding public health at large.

### Ⅲ . Local Context vs. Global Framework
* How does this massive global goal manifest within the specific, local community you are working in?

Within our target township communities, these global goals manifest not as abstract concepts, but as a direct shield for the **local healthcare safety net**. Township hospitals are the first line of medical defense for rural populations.

When SDGs 3, 8, and 12 manifest locally:

- **Local SDG 3 & 8:** It means a rural radiologist or nurse can work a 30-year career serving their neighbors without developing preventable, radiation-induced chronic illnesses due to a lack of local oversight.
    
- **Local SDG 12:** It means that hazardous radioactive waste generated from localized diagnostics is strictly contained within the facility, ensuring that agricultural soil and local water tables—which rural families rely on daily—never suffer from medical contamination.
    
Essentially, the global goals manifest here as the vital stabilization of rural healthcare infrastructure. If township doctors get sick or leave due to unsafe working conditions, the local community loses its immediate access to healthcare.


* What distinct local dynamics make this challenge unique or different from the global narrative?

The global narrative surrounding radiation safety in rural or developing regions usually centers on a lack of funding and outdated, poorly maintained equipment. However, the distinct dynamic in these township hospitals is a striking paradox: **the hardware is relatively advanced, but safety awareness is lacking.**

- **The Hardware-Awareness Disconnect:** Local government investments have successfully equipped these township facilities with modern, high-tier diagnostic machinery (such as advanced multi-slice CT scanners). However, the specialized training and safety culture have not trickled down at the same pace. The core challenge is not upgrading the machines, but upgrading the _human habits_ surrounding them.
    
- **Complacency Born of Automation:** Because the machines are new, digital, and sleek, staff often possess a false sense of security. There is a local perception that "new machines are automatically safe," which breeds complacency. This manifests in staff neglecting basic physical safeguards—such as failing to wear personal dosimeter badges, leaving lead-lined doors cracked open during scans, or neglecting to provide thyroid shields to patients' families who step into the room.
    
- **The "Invisible Risk" Factor in Rural Medicine:** In these communities, clinical focus is heavily consumed by immediate, visible patient needs and high daily volumes. Because radiation is an invisible, cumulative hazard with no immediate feedback loop (unlike a needle stick or an infectious disease), it is easily deprioritized in a low-awareness environment. Our project must therefore focus on psychological and cultural interventions to make an invisible risk visible to the local workforce.

### Ⅳ . Interconnections & Trade-offs
* Are there secondary SDGs that your project touches or impacts?
*
Yes, bridging the awareness gap around advanced machinery creates a positive domino effect across three secondary goals. It directly supports **SDG 4 (Quality Education)** by translating dense radiological standards into accessible, daily workplace training for rural medical staff. It advances **SDG 9 (Sustainable Infrastructure)** by ensuring that state-funded, high-tech diagnostic hardware is paired with proper human management protocols, maximizing its long-term utility. Finally, it addresses **SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities)** by elevating the safety standards of rural township clinics to match urban tiers, ensuring that under-represented rural workers and patients receive the same health protections as their urban counterparts.

* Could rapid progress in your primary goal create unintended strains or trade-offs in another area (e.g., economic vs. environmental balances)?

Aggressively enforcing strict radiation compliance on a low-awareness workforce can create a severe operational bottleneck and a financial strain on the hospital's budget. Mechanically forcing overworked, multi-role staff to complete rigid safety checklists slows down patient throughput, directly restricting immediate medical access for rural patients. Furthermore, a sudden fear of non-compliance can cause staff to over-classify standard trash as hazardous radioactive waste out of caution; this artificially inflates waste disposal volumes, draining limited township hospital funds away from essential medical supplies and patient care.

### Ⅴ . Defining Meaningful Contribution
* You cannot solve an entire SDG in six weeks. What does a realistic, grounded, and unexaggerated contribution look like within your timeframe?

- **The Baseline Friction Map:** An audit document in line with GBZ 130-2020 that explicitly points out where the high-tech machinery's user interface or layout accidentally encourages lazy safety habits (e.g., "The CT scanner control desk faces away from the warning light, so technicians don't notice if it fails").
    
- **Point-of-Use Nudges (The Strategy):** Developing highly localized, visual tools that bridge the awareness gap right at the machine. This includes creating localized "10-Second Room Reset" posters or color-coded waste bin placards that match the exact layout of that specific township clinic.




* What qualitative or quantitative indicators will you look for to prove this contribution?

#### Quantitative Indicators 

- **The "Locked Door" Ratio:** The percentage of times a radiation room door is found fully closed and latched during active scans before your audit versus the final week of your project.
    
- **Badge Compliance Rate:** The number of multi-role medical staff physically wearing their personal dosimeter badges on their collars during a random afternoon checkpoint, compared to the baseline day.
    
- **Waste Over-Classification Volume:** The physical volume (or weight) of standard, non-hazardous medical trash mistakenly thrown into radioactive decay bins. A decrease proves that awareness training has stopped "defensive over-sorting."
    

#### Qualitative Indicators 

- **The "Invisible Risk" Vocabulary:** In interviews, tracking whether staff still refer to the advanced machinery as "automatically safe" because it is new, or if they can articulate the specific scatter-radiation paths unique to that room layout.
    
- **SOP Friction Level:** Observing technicians run a patient diagnostic using your new long-term strategy templates. If they can seamlessly execute the safety checks without it adding more than 60 seconds of delay to the patient queue, you have proven operational viability.
